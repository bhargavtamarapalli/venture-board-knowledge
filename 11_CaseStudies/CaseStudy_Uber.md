# Case Study: Uber

**Version:** 1.0

## Purpose

Analyze Uber's transformation from a ride-hailing startup into a global mobility platform. This case study focuses on marketplace dynamics, network effects, operational execution and scalable growth strategies that can be applied to evaluating marketplace startups.

---

## Company Snapshot

**Company:** Uber

**Founded:** 2009

**Industry:** Mobility / Marketplace

**Business Model:** Two-sided marketplace connecting riders and drivers while earning commissions on completed trips.

---

## Problem

Urban transportation was often unreliable, inconvenient and lacked transparency. Customers struggled to quickly find available taxis, while drivers had inefficient utilization and limited demand visibility.

---

## Initial Validation

Uber launched with a narrow focus:

- Premium ride requests
- Smartphone-based booking
- Cashless payments
- Real-time driver tracking

Early customer adoption validated demand for convenient on-demand transportation before expanding to broader markets.

---

## Product Strategy

The MVP focused on one core workflow:

- Request a ride in minutes.

Initial capabilities included:

- Rider app
- Driver app
- GPS tracking
- Fare estimation
- Digital payments

Additional mobility services such as food delivery and freight were introduced after establishing market leadership.

---

## Go-To-Market Strategy

Uber expanded city by city.

Growth drivers included:

- Referral incentives
- Driver acquisition programs
- Dynamic pricing
- Mobile-first experience
- Local market launches

Marketplace liquidity became the primary scaling objective.

---

## Business Model

Revenue sources:

- Trip commissions
- Surge pricing
- Uber One subscriptions
- Delivery services
- Freight and logistics

The marketplace model scaled without owning transportation assets.

---

## Competitive Advantages

Uber built durable advantages through:

- Marketplace network effects
- Strong consumer brand
- Driver supply network
- Routing and pricing algorithms
- Large operational dataset
- Global geographic presence

These advantages strengthened as marketplace liquidity improved.

---

## Risks Faced

- Regulatory challenges
- Driver classification
- Marketplace imbalance
- Safety concerns
- Local competition
- Profitability pressures

Operational improvements and geographic diversification reduced long-term risk.

---

## Key Metrics

Examples of important metrics:

- Monthly Active Riders
- Active Drivers
- Gross Bookings
- Completed Trips
- Marketplace Liquidity
- Driver Utilization
- Customer Retention

---

## Lessons for Startup Evaluation

- Solve a high-frequency customer problem.
- Build liquidity before expanding aggressively.
- Network effects require balanced supply and demand.
- Operational excellence is a competitive advantage.
- Geographic expansion should follow repeatable execution.

---

## Evaluation Questions

- Can marketplace liquidity be achieved?
- Does every additional participant improve platform value?
- Can operations scale efficiently?
- Are switching costs increasing?
- Can adjacent services increase lifetime value?
