# Competitive Moat Analysis Framework

**Version:** 1.0

## Purpose

Evaluate whether a startup can build and sustain long-term competitive advantages that protect it from competitors and create durable business value. A strong moat increases pricing power, customer retention, profitability, and investor attractiveness.

---

## When To Use

- Startup idea validation
- Investor due diligence
- Product strategy
- Business model evaluation
- Fundraising preparation
- Competitive risk assessment

---

## Inputs Required

- Business model
- Product strategy
- Customer research
- Competitor analysis
- Technology stack
- Distribution strategy
- Intellectual property information

---

## Core Concepts

A competitive moat is a structural advantage that allows a company to maintain superior performance over competitors for an extended period.

Strong moats become more valuable as the business scales and make competitive replication increasingly difficult.

Common moat categories include:

- Network Effects
- Brand
- Switching Costs
- Proprietary Technology
- Data Advantage
- Economies of Scale
- Distribution Advantage
- Community
- Regulatory Advantage
- Operational Excellence

---

## Framework

### Step 1 — Identify Existing Advantages

Document every current competitive advantage.

---

### Step 2 — Classify the Moat

Determine whether each advantage belongs to:

- Technology
- Brand
- Distribution
- Cost
- Data
- Community
- Customer Lock-in
- Regulatory

---

### Step 3 — Evaluate Strength

Assess each moat using:

- Difficulty to replicate
- Time required to build
- Capital required
- Sustainability
- Customer impact

---

### Step 4 — Assess Durability

Determine how the moat evolves over time.

Questions include:

- Does it strengthen with scale?
- Does customer usage reinforce it?
- Can incumbents easily copy it?

---

### Step 5 — Identify Weaknesses

Document areas where the startup lacks defensibility.

Recommend actions to strengthen long-term competitive positioning.

---

## Evaluation Checklist

- Clear differentiation exists
- Competitive advantage supported by evidence
- Difficult for competitors to copy
- Improves customer retention
- Supports pricing power
- Strengthens over time

---

## Questions To Ask

- Why can't competitors replicate this?
- Does the moat improve as the company grows?
- Does it create switching costs?
- Does it increase customer loyalty?
- How long will this advantage remain valuable?

---

## Metrics

- Customer Retention
- Net Revenue Retention
- Switching Cost
- Brand Awareness
- Network Growth
- Gross Margin
- Market Share
- Customer Lifetime Value

---

## Red Flags

- No identifiable moat
- Competing only on price
- Easy product replication
- Temporary technological advantage
- Weak customer loyalty

---

## Best Practices

- Build multiple complementary moats.
- Reinforce advantages through continuous execution.
- Validate defensibility using customer and competitor research.
- Review moat strength periodically.

---

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing features with moats.
- Assuming first-mover advantage is permanent.
- Ignoring distribution advantages.
- Underestimating incumbent responses.

---

## Output Format

Produce:

- Moat Assessment
- Moat Classification
- Strength Rating
- Competitive Risks
- Recommendations
- Overall Defensibility Score
